Section 01 · Industry 101

What is customer support, actually?

For most of the 20th century, customer support was a cost centre — a ticket came in, someone answered a phone, the ticket went away. The number that mattered was cost per contact. Nothing else.

In the last fifteen years, two things changed. First, subscription businesses (SaaS, streaming, D2C) discovered that keeping a customer is 5–7× cheaper than acquiring one — so anything that affects retention, like support quality, suddenly mattered in rupee terms. Second, CRM systems started unifying customer data, so for the first time a support interaction could be seen in the context of the customer's whole relationship with the brand — how much they've bought, how satisfied they've been, how close they are to churning.

Modern customer support is really three functions stitched together, and the analyst's job is to measure all three honestly:

Operations

Answer the ticket

Staffing, scheduling, routing, SLAs, cost per contact. The legacy "call centre" function — still critical, still a large P&L line.

Experience

Make it feel good

CSAT, NPS, sentiment, tone. How the customer feels when the ticket is closed. The bridge between operations and retention.

Retention

Turn it into loyalty

Churn signals, LTV preservation, upsell at the moment of truth. Support as a revenue function, not just a cost one.

◆ Why this matters for you

A weak analyst reports the operational layer only: "AHT went up, CSAT went down." A good analyst connects it to the retention layer: "AHT went up because of a product defect that also drove CSAT down in the Pulse Pro cohort, and those customers are now 4× more likely to churn. Fixing the defect preserves ₹X of LTV." That bridge is what earns a seat at the table.

In-house vs BPO — the eternal debate

Every D2C brand hits this crossroads around ₹100 Cr ARR: your support volume is now too big for an in-house team, but outsourcing it to a BPO feels like giving up control of the customer relationship. There's no universally right answer. The table below is what a typical trade-off looks like — and the SoundWave case is specifically designed to make you think about this.

DimensionIn-houseBPO (Outsourced)

Section 03 · The Mental Model

CRM tiers — the pyramid that runs the business

Every CRM-savvy business segments customers into tiers based on value. The pyramid shape isn't an accident — it reflects a brutal reality: the top 5–10% of your customers often contribute 30–50% of revenue, and losing one of them hurts exponentially more than losing a bottom-tier customer. Support resources must be allocated asymmetrically.

PLATINUM
Top 5%Highest LTV, longest tenure, usually brand advocates. White-glove support. Retention cost is small compared to what you lose if they leave.
GOLD
Next 15–20%Strong repeat purchasers. Target for upsell to Platinum. Priority routing on support tickets; personalized outreach.
SILVER
Middle 40%Standard customers. Cost-efficient support. Segment most exposed to churn — and where retention programs have biggest ROI.
BRONZE
Bottom 35–40%One-time or low-value buyers. Self-serve-first support. The honest question: are you profitable serving them?
★ The analyst's most common mistake

Reporting a single blended CSAT, churn rate, or AHT across all customers and declaring "we have a problem." Almost always, the problem is concentrated in 1–2 tiers and the fix belongs there, not across the board. Segment before you conclude. Always.

Lifecycle stages — where they are in their journey

CRM tiers tell you how valuable a customer is today. Lifecycle stages tell you how they're moving. A "Loyal" Gold customer and an "At-Risk" Gold customer need completely different interventions, even though they're in the same tier.

StageDefinitionWhat Support Should Do
NewFirst 30 days after signupProactive onboarding outreach. First impression = everything.
At-Risk New30–120 days, only 1 orderSecond-order nudge. This is where most churn actually happens.
ActiveRecent purchase, 2+ ordersStandard service. Keep it boring. Don't break what's working.
Loyal3+ orders, high tier, recent activityVIP treatment. Priority routing. Personalized touches.
DormantNo activity in 90+ daysWin-back campaigns. Understand why they left.
Section 04 · From business problem to data answer

The real questions CX leaders ask — and how an analyst translates them

A VP of CX doesn't walk in and say "please calculate the weighted FCR segmented by agent tenure." They say things like "Why are customers angry?" Your job is to translate that into a sharp, answerable data question, run the numbers, and translate the answer back into a business decision. Every row below is a real question we've heard. The SoundWave case is essentially the first 4 of these, rolled together.

1. Business Problem

"Why is CSAT down this quarter?" — VP CX, after a board review

Data Question

Decompose the quarter's CSAT into channel, centre, reason, tier, and SLA-breach drivers; quantify the contribution of each to the overall movement.

Analytical Approach

Weighted-mean decomposition. Compare this quarter vs last on each dimension. Isolate mix-shift effects from rate-shift effects.

Decision it Drives

Where to focus the fix — centre retraining, SLA staffing, or a specific product issue.

2. Business Problem

"Are our BPO centres worth keeping?" — COO doing budget planning

Data Question

Compare cost-per-contact vs CSAT vs FCR across in-house and BPO centres, normalized for reason/tier mix.

Analytical Approach

Centre-level scorecard. Cost-adjusted CSAT. If you pay ₹150/contact for BPO but lose ₹80 of LTV from lower CSAT, net cost is ₹230 — not ₹150.

Decision it Drives

Which centres to renew, re-negotiate, or bring in-house.

3. Business Problem

"We need a self-serve strategy." — CFO looking at rising support cost

Data Question

What % of tickets could plausibly be deflected to self-serve, and what's the cost savings vs CSAT trade-off at different deflection rates?

Analytical Approach

Top-reason analysis: identify reasons that are high-volume, low-complexity, and low-CSAT-risk (e.g. refund status, order tracking).

Decision it Drives

Which ticket reasons to automate first. Investment case for the bot or help centre.

4. Business Problem

"Who is about to churn, and why?" — Head of Retention, every single week

Data Question

Build a customer-level churn-risk score from support behaviour (ticket count, CSAT, SLA breaches, escalations, NPS, tier, subscription).

Analytical Approach

Feature engineering, weighting (regression or scorecard), validation against historical churn. Produce a ranked list with reasons.

Decision it Drives

Which 100 accounts to call this week. Which interventions to offer which risk tiers.

5. Business Problem

"Our NPS dropped 8 points. Is it real?" — Brand Manager, panicked

Data Question

Is the NPS drop driven by sampling noise, a real shift in detractor share, or a mix shift in who responded?

Analytical Approach

Decompose NPS change into response-rate-adjusted detractor %. Check respondent demographics vs customer base.

Decision it Drives

Whether to escalate as a CX crisis or let it ride for another cycle.

6. Business Problem

"Are our new agents productive?" — WFM manager defending training spend

Data Question

What's the productivity + quality curve by tenure? How many months until a new agent reaches parity with a senior one?

Analytical Approach

Cohort analysis by hire month. Plot AHT, FCR, CSAT against tenure.

Decision it Drives

Training investment, promotion pathways, bonus structure.

7. Business Problem

"This product line is generating too many tickets." — Head of Product, defensive

Data Question

Which product SKUs have the highest ticket-per-unit-sold rate, and is it a product quality issue, a documentation issue, or an expectations mismatch?

Analytical Approach

Ticket rate per 1,000 units sold, by SKU. Reason-mix within each SKU. Compare to product-team's claimed defect rate.

Decision it Drives

Whether to recall, fix firmware, re-label, or just improve the setup guide.

8. Business Problem

"Is our SoundWave+ subscription worth running?" — CMO defending the programme

Data Question

Do subscribers have better retention, CSAT, and LTV than non-subscribers, controlling for tier? What's the incremental LTV of the subscription?

Analytical Approach

Matched cohort comparison (subscriber vs non-subscriber) within each tier. Subtract subscription cost from LTV uplift.

Decision it Drives

Scale the programme, kill it, or reprice.

Great CX analysts don't just answer "what happened to the ticket." They answer "what happened to the customer." The ticket is a snapshot. The customer is a movie. — An anonymous Head of CX at a unicorn, on what separates great analysts
Section 05 · The Language

KPI Dictionary — the words you must know cold

Every metric below shows up in the SoundWave case. Master these before you touch the data. Click to expand.

Experience metrics

Customer Satisfaction (CSAT)
CSAT = avg(rating) on a 1–5 scale (sometimes expressed as % giving 4 or 5)

Post-interaction survey asking "how was your experience." The fundamental experience metric. Response rates are usually 60–80%; beware non-response bias (angry customers are both more and less likely to respond depending on the moment).

Benchmark (D2C India): 4.2 overall, 72% Promoter share (4–5).
Net Promoter Score (NPS)
NPS = % Promoters (9–10) − % Detractors (0–6) (range −100 to +100)

"How likely are you to recommend us to a friend?" on a 0–10 scale. Relationship-level loyalty metric, distinct from post-interaction CSAT. NPS is the single best leading indicator of churn at the customer level.

Benchmark: +38 for D2C electronics; +50+ is world-class.
Customer Effort Score (CES)
CES = avg(response) to "How much effort did you have to put in?" on a 1–7 scale

The quieter cousin of CSAT/NPS. Research shows effort predicts loyalty better than satisfaction — a customer can be "satisfied" with a resolved issue but still churn if the process was exhausting.

Operational metrics

Average Handle Time (AHT)
AHT = Talk Time + Hold Time + After-Call Wrap-up Time

Minutes per interaction. The classic productivity metric. Warning: optimizing AHT too hard hurts CSAT and FCR — agents rush to close tickets. Always look at AHT alongside quality metrics, never alone.

Benchmark: 14 min voice, 9 min chat, 24 min email (includes drafting).
First Contact Resolution (FCR)
FCR = Tickets resolved without re-contact / Total tickets × 100%

The gold-standard quality metric. A high-FCR ticket costs less, produces higher CSAT, and doesn't generate a second ticket. Most CX teams will sacrifice a few minutes of AHT for a higher FCR — the math pays off.

Benchmark: 68% for D2C electronics.
Average Speed of Answer (ASA)
ASA = avg(time from queue entry to agent pickup) in seconds (voice)

How long the customer waited before a human picked up. Staffing adequacy's most direct measure. Abandonment rate is ASA's sibling — customers who wait too long hang up.

Benchmark: 45 sec voice, 60 sec chat; 80/20 rule = 80% answered within 20 sec.
Abandonment Rate
Abandonment = Calls dropped before answered / Calls offered × 100%

% of customers who hang up before the agent picks up. Direct signal of understaffing or poor IVR. Industry rule of thumb: > 10% is a problem, < 5% is healthy.

SLA Attainment
SLA % = Tickets meeting SLA / Total tickets × 100%

Contractual or promised response/resolution time window met. Different SLAs for different channels (voice: seconds, email: hours). The gap between SLA targets and delivery is often where CSAT quietly bleeds.

Schedule Adherence & Occupancy
Adherence = Time worked as scheduled / Scheduled time
Occupancy = Time handling contacts / Logged-in time

Two workforce metrics that live together. Adherence tells you if agents are at their desks. Occupancy tells you if they're working when they are. Both > 85% signals a well-run centre; both < 75% signals slack.

Cost metrics

Cost per Contact (CPC)
CPC = Total centre cost / Contacts handled

Fully-loaded cost: agent wages + supervisor + infra + overhead. Voice is the most expensive channel; self-serve is 10–30× cheaper. This metric is why every CFO wants a deflection strategy.

Benchmark: ₹320 voice, ₹140 chat, ₹12 self-serve.
Cost to Serve (CTS)
CTS per customer = Annual support cost for that customer / Revenue from that customer

The tier-aware efficiency metric. A Bronze customer generating 3 tickets a month on a ₹1,200 AOV is probably unprofitable. The most sophisticated CX orgs measure CTS and tier their support accordingly.

Retention metrics

Churn Rate
Churn % = Customers lost in period / Customers at start × 100%

The big number. Usually expressed monthly or annually. For subscription businesses, even a 1% monthly churn compounds to 11.4% annually — small numbers matter.

Post-Support Churn Rate (30-day)
Post-Support Churn % = Customers with a ticket who churn within 30 days / Total customers with a ticket

The single most telling metric linking support to retention. A healthy number is 3–5%. Above 8% means something is structurally broken in your support experience.

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) & LTV:CAC
LTV = AOV × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan
LTV:CAC = LTV / Customer Acquisition Cost

The unit-economics backbone. LTV:CAC > 3 is healthy. Support affects LTV through both lifespan (churn reduction) and frequency (satisfied customers buy more). A 5% retention improvement typically drives 25–95% more profit (Bain & Co classic).

Repeat Contact Rate
Repeat Contact % = Customers with 4+ tickets in 90 days / Total customers with any ticket

The under-appreciated churn predictor. A customer who needs to contact you 4+ times in 90 days has almost always given up emotionally, even if each individual ticket closed "fine."

Section 06 · The Role

A day in the life of a CX analyst

Here's a composite Tuesday. Almost none of this looks like the glamorous "data science" work that job postings promise. Most of it is translation: fuzzy business questions in, sharp numeric answers out, business recommendations back.

08:50
Open laptop. CSAT dashboard shows yesterday at 3.82 — below the 4.20 benchmark for the fourth day running. Slack is already on fire.
09:15
Daily stand-up. Report yesterday's numbers. VP-CX asks for a breakdown by centre by EOD. You nod — you already know where this is going.
10:00
Pull ticket data, segment by centre and channel. Pune BPO's CSAT has dropped to 3.5; in-house Bangalore is still at 4.0. Check reason mix — Pune is suddenly handling 40% more "Product Defect" tickets this week.
11:30
Ping product team on Slack. "Is there a firmware issue with Pulse Pro released last week?" They go quiet for 20 minutes. Then confirm: a firmware update caused random Bluetooth drops.
12:45
Lunch at desk. The root cause isn't support — it's product. Start drafting a note: CSAT drop is product-driven, not centre-driven.
14:00
Build the weekly churn-risk list for retention. Run your scorecard. 87 customers flagged HIGH, up from 62 last week. The uptick tracks the Pulse Pro issue.
15:15
Head of Retention calls. "Can you call out the top-10 Platinum accounts?" You filter your list. 2 of them are at-risk. She calls them personally that afternoon with an offer. Saves both.
16:45
CFO pings: "How many of those product-defect tickets are on the ₹8,999 Studio Pro?" You check. Only 8%. Mostly ₹4,999 Pulse Pro. You share the mix. Recall scope conversation shifts.
18:00
Send EOD note to VP-CX with 3 slides: centre breakdown (exonerating Pune BPO), root-cause (product), recommended action (firmware rollback + proactive outreach). Close laptop.
★ What you actually get paid for

Notice that the biggest value the analyst created today was correctly reassigning blame. If they had just reported "Pune BPO's CSAT is down, renegotiate the contract," they would have hurt a vendor relationship AND missed the real product issue. Good CX analysts don't just answer the question they were asked — they answer the question someone should have asked.

CX analyst is one of the most optionality-rich seats in analytics. Section 08 · Toolkit

The tools you'll actually use

Like every analyst role, the stated stack and the actual stack are different. Here's the honest breakdown by how much of your time each tool eats.

  1. SQL — 35–45% of your time. Non-negotiable. Every ticketing system and CRM sits on top of a warehouse (Redshift, BigQuery, Snowflake, Databricks). Window functions, CTEs, joins cold.
  2. Excel / Google Sheets — 25–35%. Yes, in 2025. Pivot tables, SUMIFS, XLOOKUP, scorecard builds. Most executive reports still land here.
  3. A BI tool — 10–15%. Looker, Tableau, Metabase, Power BI, Freshdesk's own dashboards. You'll build and maintain dashboards daily.
  4. Ticketing / CRM UIs — 10%. Freshdesk, Zendesk, Salesforce Service Cloud, Kapture. You'll need to pull raw exports and understand the data model.
  5. Python / Notebooks — 5–10%. Pandas and scikit-learn for churn models, text analysis on ticket descriptions, NPS verbatim clustering.
  6. Presentation & storytelling — always. The analysts who grow fastest are the ones who can turn a 50-row pivot into a three-slide narrative a VP can act on.
◆ The honest advice no one gives you

The CX analysts who get promoted quickly are not the ones who master the most tools. They're the ones who understand the customer. Read 30 tickets a week. Listen to 5 calls a month. Know what it feels like when an angry customer gets their issue closed. You'll find patterns in the data that pure number-crunchers miss because you'll know what to look for.

Section 09 · Approach

How to actually tackle the SoundWave case

You now know enough context. Here's the sequence we'd follow if we were opening the workbook fresh. The capstone churn model is designed to be built last — don't be tempted to start there.

  1. Read the case doc twice, cover-to-cover. First pass: absorb the context and stakeholders. Second pass: underline each specific analysis question and required metric. Don't open the Excel yet.
  2. Write the 3-line executive summary you hope to end with. Before touching data, write your hypothesis. e.g. "Q3 CSAT dipped because of a product defect in Pulse Pro. BPO centres show lower CSAT than in-house. Top churn risk sits in Bronze non-subscribers. We recommend firmware rollback, an LTV-weighted retention push on 100 HIGH-risk customers, and a 5-pt self-serve shift for ₹1.4 Cr annual savings."
  3. Clean the data — honestly and visibly. Unify date formats, normalize gender casing, standardize city spellings, handle \N/nulls. Document every cleaning step. This alone is worth 10% of your grade.
  4. Build the diagnostic views. Overall scorecard first, then drill: channel, centre, reason, tier, lifecycle, SLA. Every view compared to benchmark.
  5. Layer in cost. Cost-per-contact by channel. Cost-per-CSAT-point by centre. Deflection opportunity quantified.
  6. Build the retention view. Churn by tier, NPS bucket, subscription status. Churn reason mix. Which support patterns correlate with historical churn.
  7. Now build the capstone model. Features → weights → score → validate → top-100 list. Write the methodology note.
  8. Synthesize → 3-bullet executive summary. Compare to your Step-2 hypothesis. The gap is where your real insight lives.
  9. Build the deck. Follow the structure in the instructions PDF. 12–16 slides, no more. Every chart with a "so-what" title.
  10. Sleep on it. Re-read. Cut 30%. The third draft is always the best.
◆ Final nudge

Every ticket in this dataset represents a real moment of frustration for a real customer (even if the customer is synthetic). The analysts who get the best grades — and the best jobs — are the ones who never forget there's a human at the other end. Numbers tell you what happened. Empathy tells you why. You need both. Good luck.
